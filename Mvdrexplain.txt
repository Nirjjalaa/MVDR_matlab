Imagine you have a 64-element antenna array.

Each antenna receives:

Desired radar echo

Interference (jammers)

Noise


So every antenna hears a mixture.

Element 2 : Target + Jammer + Noise
Element 3 : Target + Jammer + Noise

x_2\\
\vdots\\
x_{64}
\end{bmatrix}

Each  is a complex number containing amplitude and phase.

What do we want?

We want to combine all 64 antenna outputs into one signal.

We assign one weight to each antenna.

Antenna1 ---- w1 ----\
Antenna2 ---- w2 -----\
Antenna3 ---- w3 ------> Sum ---> Output
...
Antenna64 --- w64 ----/

Mathematically

where

w = weights we must calculate


The entire beamforming problem is simply:

> How do we choose the best weights?

Step 4 — The Constraint

MVDR must never distort the target.

So

\mathbf w^H
\mathbf a(\theta)
=1

Meaning

If the desired signal arrives,

Output amplitude remains exactly 1.

Target passes unchanged.


---

Why do we need this constraint?

Without it, the optimization would choose

w = 0

because zero weights produce zero output power—the smallest possible value.

But then the target disappears too.

So we force the algorithm to keep the target.

\mathbf w

Since the target is constrained to pass unchanged, minimizing total output power effectively suppresses everything except the desired signal.

Step 6 — Why the inverse ?

This is the most important idea.

Imagine

Road C
2 cars

If you want the fastest route, you avoid busy roads.

Similarly,

High covariance = lots of interference.

Low covariance = cleaner direction.

The inverse covariance matrix gives less emphasis to directions with high power/correlation and more emphasis to cleaner directions.

Conceptually:

Large covariance
↓

Inverse becomes small
↓

Weight reduced

Small covariance
↓

Inverse becomes larger
↓

Weight increased

Step 7 — Final Weight Equation

\boxed{
\mathbf w=
\frac{\mathbf R^{-1}\mathbf a}
{\mathbf a^H\mathbf R^{-1}\mathbf a}
}

Let's interpret it.

Numerator

R^{-1}a

Take the desired steering vector and modify it according to the interference environment.


---

Denominator

a^H R^{-1} a

This is a normalization factor.

Without it, the gain toward the target could become greater or less than 1.

Dividing by this value guarantees

w^H a=1

so the desired signal remains undistorted.


---

Why is MVDR called "Minimum Variance Distortionless Response"?

Each word has a meaning:

Minimum → as small as possible.

Variance → output power (mostly interference + noise).

Distortionless → target is not altered because .

Response → the beamformer output.


So the full meaning is:

> Find the beamformer weights that produce the minimum output power while ensuring the desired signal passes through unchanged.

The intuition to remember forever

Imagine you're asking 64 friends to identify a person in a crowd.

Friend 1 has perfect eyesight.

Friend 20 is standing in heavy smoke.

Friend 45 is facing the wrong direction.


Would you trust all 64 equally?

No.

You listen more to the reliable friends and less to the unreliable ones.

That is exactly what the covariance matrix tells MVDR:

> "Which measurements are reliable, and which are corrupted?"



The inverse covariance matrix then converts that knowledge into smaller influence for unreliable measurements and greater influence for reliable ones, while the denominator finally rescales everything so that the desired target is passed without any distortion ().

Once this intuition is solid, the equation stops looking like a mysterious formula and starts looking like a logical sequence:

1. Know where the target should look ().


2. Discount contaminated measurements ().


3. Rescale so the target's gain stays exactly one ().
